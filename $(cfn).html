STAGEFRIGHT

Barbara Fifer

My first appearance in public was a trying or-deal. I was playing in a piano recital and there were about fifty other students beside myself who were also playing in the recital. I was eleven years old and thoroughly frightened. I was to play a waltz and a march and was about the fortieth student to play in the program.

    Seeing the big audience made me shudder in my tracks and I was in a terrible suspense waiting for my turn to play. When my turn finally came, I slowly and quite unsteadily, to be sure, made my way across the stage to the piano. My teeth were chattering and suddenly my fingers became very stiff! Everyone was watching me, so I tried to begin to play. Somehow I finished the waltz and was waiting for the audience to become quiet in order that I could finish and run off the stage from all these frightening people. I played the march as if I were chasing a fire engine to a fire, and almost fell, because I was in such a hurry to leave the stage.

     My next public appearance came when I learned how to play the violin and joined the Irving Junior High School orchestra. I also played in the Lin-coln High School Summer "A" orchestra.

     The most embarassing [embarrassing] situation arose while I was playing in a concert at Northeast High School. It happened just before curtain time. I broke a violin string and was unable to fix it myself. Our conductor finally fixed it for me delaying the pro-gram about a half minute.

1946/1946_05.jpg

     My second piano recital was a little more like recitals ought to be. However, I was so scared my hands became cold, and I could not move my fingers. The agony and suspense of waiting for my turn to play in the program was dreadful. I was contin-uously trying to tell myself that I might profit by listening to the others play. The main diffi-culty was sitting still without making any noises and not being conspicuous. I was supposed to be enjoying the music! My main accomplishment of this recital was that there was a more distinct difference between my march and other number.

     Playing at home also presets its problems. I do not know of anyone who likes to listen to any-one practice piano. This is particularly true at our house. I am terribly annoyed when I know that other people are listening to me practice and wat-ching my play. The only audience that does not other me is- - -Mom. She usually sits and knits while she listens to me play. She criticizes my playing, but it does not seem to bother me.

     Last year I played violin at Teachers College High School. We called our small class of three students a "String Trio." Because of the size of the class, we played solos almost every day, and I was gradually beginning to lose some of my stage-fright.

     No matter how often I play in public, I always have at least a mild case of- - - - - - - - - -Stagefright.

MY CHRISTMAS MEMORIES

Howard Bingham

Some of the happiest memories of my childhood are centered around Christmas time. We always have a Christmas tree with gifts when we get up Christmas morning.

     My mother recalls one Christmas when she sent my brothers and me back to bed four times during the night to finally have us awake the whole household about five a.m. I remember the high-light for me on this particular Christmas was a red wagon.

     After eating breakfast and examining our gifts left by Santa, we always went to my grandmother's house for a noon-time Christmas dinner of roast turkey, goose, or duck with cranberries and dress-ing, mince and pumpkin pie, fruit cake made by Grandmother, and everything in the way of food any one can imagine. All my cousins, brothers, and sisters, and even the grownups, ate until they were practically unconscious.

     Then we op-ened gifts which the relatives always exchanged. Each year the procedure was the same. Then all the young folks went out sleigh riding, skating, or play-ing games while the women washed the dishes after dinner.

     Since we are now older, we celebrate Christ-mas in our own home, often with a lot of aunts, uncles, and cous-ins helping us.

1946/1946_08.jpg

     This will be the first Christmas any member of our family of ten has had to be away from home, but I know my brother, who is away, will have a lot of pleasant memories of past Christmases. We all hope he will be home again by next Christmas.

BOYS WHO SMOKE

Martha Webb

All races have found something to smoke. People down through the ages have been smoking powdered herbs, grasses, and medical powders. Indians were the first to smoke tobacco. They taught the colo-nists how to raise and use it.

     Young people have been given a healthy body and talents. When they take to smoking they are wast-ing health, talent, and money. Young people are more susceptible to injuries from smoking than are adults. Those who smoke never live as long as those who do not. Smoking shortens their wind and causes a cough. It also breaks down their health. The latter isn't something money can buy after it is lost.

     Boys can't impress other people by smoking. Anyone can learn to smoke- - even a five year old. It's not an art, as some boys would like us to believe.

     To my way of thinking there are three distinct types of youthful smokers:

     Those who say:

     "I'm tough, see? I'm fifteen years old, but you think I look twenty-one?"

     Number two: The type who act like Humphrey Bogart and take long drags at their cigarettes and manufacture a tough look in their eyes.

     Number three: They type who act as if they have been smoking all their lives, when in reality only lately has it become a habit.

     They don't fool us a bit and it doesn't make them look more manly. Besides, who wants to be 1946/1946_10.jpg near a boy who smells like a smoke stack. They look rather silly with a cigarette dangling out of the corner of their mouth. Anyway we prefer the wholesome Van Johnson type. We don't like to see boys start bad habits which will last them all their lives.

TRIPPING THE LIGHT FANTASTIC

(Consisting of a critical analysis of the positions most commonly used therein)

Twyla Dobbins

Dancing is a great American sport. It provides entertainment not only for the people who partici-pate bus also for those who watch. Perhaps it is even more enjoyable to be among the watchers.

     I have often wished I were an orchestra member who could travel over the national playing for dan-ces. Then I could observe the different positions used in sections other than my own locality. As it is, I shall have to content myself with a de-tailed description of the dancing postures with which I am familiar.

     Some couples use the far-away position. It is formed thus: The couple stand as far apart as possible, close their eyes, reach out and grab for each other's waist and shoulder. The results are disastrous! There they are, dancing away- - -but the way they look! It's as good as a movie to watch them. About four feet apart, they struggle to get farther apart but succeed only in making certain parts of their anatomy protrude in a very undignified manner. This version of the dance is very common.

     There is also the "cuddle-up" position which is usually practised [practiced] by c.f.'s ("educated foxes," more commonly known as "wolves.") This is just the opposite of the far-away position. The man tries to choke the girl, while she, not to be out-done, proves her possession of a superwoman grip by clutching his shoulder tightly. Instead of im-pressing the innocent by-stander with the beauty 1946/1946_12.jpg of their passion, they seem to be in the throes of a violent antipathy. Perhaps they believe their attitude appears to be that of lovers- -but oh, brother!

     Dancing on one's feet is a good idea. So many people seem to do it. However, dancing on someone else's feet is also a very common practice. The latter is not particularly popular with the person to whom the feet belong. For some reason, each individual likes to dance on his own feet, but does not care to have others dance on them as well.

     In order to avoid stepping on other dancers' feet, many participants in the sport take long, sweeping steps. They may think they look graceful, but they really resemble nothing so much as assor-ted ostriches walking about the dance floor. This method usually eliminates the problem of stepping on people's feet, but who wants to look like an ostrich?

     One frequently meets the boy who is obviously doing a dance of duty. His mouth is set in a firm, unpleasant line; and, frowning menacingly at one and all, he pushes his unhappy partner around the floor as if she wore a sack of meal.

     Then there is the couple who seem utterly ob-livious to everything and everyone around them. As they gaze soulfully at each other, their eyes gradually droop shut, and they imagine themselves in Heaven. The other dancers express their dis-approval by frowning and muttering. Finally, the "out of this world" couple are brought rudely back to earth by a series of bumps and collisions. Does this bother them? On, no! They dance ahead as if there had been no interruption. At least the other dancers retire and watch them as they drift through dreamland. The floor is theirs, and they use it, too.

1946/1946_13.jpg

     The show-off couple can be found upon any dance floor. Their main purpose is to be outlandish and different. They will dance in any way in order to make themselves the center of attention. They will execute rapid turns, hop, jump, or even turn somersaults in order to be the chief attraction. This mode of dancing is commonly called "jitter-bugging."

     Yes, dancing is fun; but it's better than a three-ring circus to watch other people trip the light fantastic.

NEGROES AND WHITES

Lee Barnhill

I used to look at a negro as a person who was sort of dirty and dangerous because I lived in the South a great deal of my life-time. Where I lived a white man who killed a negro was put in one night and let out the next morning, but if a negro killed a white, the negro was put to death immediately.

     Down South a negro has his own school, his own church and his own side of the railroad track; but here they sometimes go to school with whites. For example: the other day I saw two girls walk-ing down the road from school. They were about six and four, the older a negro, the younger a white. They had their arms around each other like a pair of sisters. Down south if a white mother saw this she probably would have had the negro's parents put in jail, which is very easy to do. I think, if we would not curse the negroes so much and let them be more like us, we would have much better luck with them. I think there would be fewer fights and killings. When I get on a bus lots of times I see a negro in one corner all by 1946/1946_14.jpg himself. The whites won't sit by him at all. They act as if they are better than he is.

     I had a very good friend once, a man about fifty years old. He was a negro. He liked me very much and would do almost anything for me. Some negroes are all right but others are just born with crook-edness. We had negroes working for us who had all come down in a truck from Austin or Houston. The whites just haul them around in trucks like horses. Wherever the driver of the truck takes them that's where they go. They live in tents, caves or right out in the open with only a couple of blankets. Their usual work is in the cotton fields, hoeing or picking.

     I think we should treat the negroes better.

MY HOME

Ruthie Merklin

My home, the State Home for Dependent Children, is located at University Place, 5903 Walker Avenue.

     Several years ago the home of Mr. and Mrs. C. C. White was bought by the State for $32,000. Be-fore this time the State Home was located on twelfth street. Since White Hall was purchased the State has added five different main buildings. Now most of the children are located on the grounds.

     White Hall is the main building. Here are lo-cated the main offices and employees' kitchen and dining room. On the third floor of White Hall the senior girls and I live. Here are also the super-intendent's apartment and welfare workers' offices.

     Next is the girls' Training Cottage. The Cot-tage is a large two story white house where girls stay for training when they become twelve years old.

1946/1946_15.jpg

     At the cottage the girls learn to cook, sew, and clean. After they are fourteen to fifteen years old, they go to White Hall. In the basement of the Cottage the fifth and sixth grade have their school room.

     Benson Hall is named after Mrs. Benson who was a chairman of the Board of Control. It consists of the main kitchen and dining room in the base-ment, the first and second nersery [nursery] on the first floor and the clinic room or doctors' offices. The sec-ond floor is where the smaller girls have their department. The third floor has a dentist's room and a hospital where all the children are isolated when they first come into the institution before they are put in a department.

     On the east side the boys have their department. In the basement of this building the first four grades have their school room. On the first and second floor the small boys from four to nine also the large boys from nine to fourteen have their department here. The third floor consists of rooms for employees.

     Besides these main buildings we have a large garage, a laundry and a school house for sixth to eighth grade. During the past two years we have added two new building, one by the school for re-creation or play and then we completed late this fall a large cave to store our canned fruit and vegetables, potatoes, cabbage, etc., we raised in the summer.

     Then out two miles we have a farm for boys from fourteen to eighteen.

     The boys have a man and wife to take care of them. They help put in the crops and harvest them, do the house cleaning, chores, get meals, and do the dishes.

     They get up around 5:30 or 6:00 and go to bed at 8:30 or 9:00.

1946/1946_16.jpg

     Mrs. Mouden, our superintendent, also bought the farm since she has been at the home.

     We also have the first nursery and second nur-sery. In the first nursery the babies are from three weeks to two years old. After they leave the first nursery they go to the second nursery where they stay from two years to four or five years, when the girls go to Benson Hall and the boys to West Side or Hager Hall.

     At the home we have seventy-two employees at the present time. That includes the file clerk, bookkeeper, and superintendent and stenographers.

     We also have plenty of entertainment; we always have parties and mixers. We always celebrate the fourth [Fourth] of July and all other holidays. In the fall we go to the Circus and State Fair.

     Well this is about all I can say, so I'll quit by saying that I am proud of my home.

JAZZ MANIACS

John Moritz

Have you ever met a maniac; a jazz maniac, I mean? They are the people who think sharps, and dance flats. All they ever think of is music. Don't get my wrong, I like music, and jazz, but not this type.

     At a dance you're dancing peacefully with a girl when somebody stops the victrola and puts on a music plate he brought from home. Out of this record comes forth sounds as though the roof were falling in.

     Two wierdly-clad [weirdly-clad] figures, maniacs, in other words, jump on to the floor, and start hitting each other over the head. Then they start some very interesting aerobatics. He grabs her, she 1946/1946_17.jpg grabs him. Wham! Big fight! They call that dan-cing. When the music stops, they drag each other off the floor and congratulate themselves on what a swell dance they had.

     You start dancing again, when who should heave into view but our bouncing buddies. They jazz to a waltz.

     After throwing you off the floor, our maniacs do a series of wiggles that would make Houdini crawl.

     Now that I've given you a general description of their dancing technique, let me show you how they talk.

     He: "How about bashin' the bundle with me, bee?"

     Silly, isn't it?

     She: "Not 'til you perambulate around to the glass gaze and slick down your mop."

     Did you ever hear such stuff? I didn't until then.

     I asked a friend of mine, who is one of those moon gazers, to go to a psychiatrist, but he re-fused.

     There's not much more to say on the topic ex-cept that the maniac piles his home full of re-cords of every type and description. He treasures each one more than the next and will murder you if you break one of the million.

     So, for quieter living, don't go near one.

ICE SKATING OR OTHERWISE

Marian Waddell

Being of sound mind (?) and body, I hereby be-queath- - - - -sounds rather pessimistic doesn't it? Well, the ice skating season is here again and I shall soon be making my yearly attempt to learn to ice skate. Therefore, before I start out, I feel a will might be a very good idea.

     Ice skating can truly be an art, when it is done properly. It is a demonstration of true grace and beauty, however, my skating is not an example of either one.

     The first time I went ice skating, I started out with high hopes; after all, it looks so simple. First, I put on my skates- -well that was easy any-way- -then I proceeded to attempt to stand up. After a fierce battle to get my skates to stop wobbling and actually get my balance, I started out. My skates seemed to have a difference of opinion concerning the direction I was going and I sat down, not very gently, I might add.

     Why something which looks so simple should be so arduous I will never understand. The fight those two little skates can put up never ceases to amaze me.

     My patience is wearing thin in the face of so many defeats, so if you see me starting out with a pair of ice skates and a "do or die" expression in my eyes, don't be alarmed, I won't kill anyone, at least not intentionally, I merely wish to learn to ice skate.

THREE GREAT DOCUMENTS IN THEHISTORY OF THE UNITED STATES

Ruby Hock

The Declaration of Independence announced the freedom of the Thirteen Colonies from Great Britain. Thomas Jefferson was responsible for the drafting of the Declaration of Independence. Many phrases were dropped from his original copy by Congress. One of the most important of these was the clause which abolished slavery in the United States. The Southern Congressmen wouldn't let this issue pass and it was therefore taken away from the document.

     The Congress studied the document on the sec-ond, third, and fourth of July. It was signed late in the afternoon on July 4, 1776. It was read publicly on the following Monday.

     The document accuses the king of trespassing up-on the rights of the people. It declares all men equal and acknowledges the right of everyone to life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness.

     The states had now thrown off the British yoke and stood without a government to hold them to-gether.

     The Articles of Confederation was the next step in the forming of our great nation. The same day the Continental Congress ordered a committee to draft the Declaration of Independence, another group was organized to prepare the Articles of Confederation. A month after the Declaration of Independence had been signed, the Articles of Con-federation were submitted to Congress for approval. This document was subjected to debate for sixteen months in Congress and even after being adopted by 1946/1946_20.jpg Congress toward the end of 1777, it was not rati-fied until March, 1781, when the war was drawing to a close

     Franklin's plan of union of 1775 was not ac-cepted by Albany Congress, but some of the most important features of the two documents are iden-tical. The Articles of Confederation provided for a Congress in which every state was entitled to one vote and was represented by a delegation whose members were appointed annually as the legislature of the state might direct and whose expenses were paid by the state and were subject to recall. In other words it was a council of states whose re-presentatives has little independence of action.

     Extensive powers were granted to the Congress. It could enter into treaties, declare war or estab-lish peace, coin money and establish post offices. But none of these powers could be exercised with-out consent of nine states and even if the consent was given, there was nothing to make the states abide by the decision of Congress.

     No executive was provided for except that Con-gress was authorized to appoint such officials as it considered necessary to run the government. Congress might be called upon to settle disputes between states and establish courts for the trial of piracy on the high seas.

     The plan of government was there but it had no force behind it. Congress might declare war, but the states might decline to participate in it; Con-gress might enter into treaties, but it could not make the states live up to it. Congress could borrow money, but it could not be sure of repaying it; and Congress could settle disputes, but it had no way of making the parties accept the decision.

     The union was ready for a stronger constitution. The Constitutional Convention was first called for the purpose of amending the Articles of Confedera-tion and there were some delegates who stayed with 1946/1946_21 this purpose throughout the whole convention. There were others, however, who realized that the Arti-cles of Confederation were not satisfactory no nomat-ter [no mat-ter] how changed they might become.

     The Constitutional Convention which met in Philadelphia in 1787 was the most important con-vention every [ever] held in the United States. The Con-federation under the Articles of Confederation was a failure and if the new nation was to be justi-fied in the eyes of the world, it must show itself capable of effective union. This Conventionand [Convention and] its outcome would mark a turning point in the his-tory of the world and all men presentwere [present were] affected by the importance of the task before them.

     All sessions of the Convention were held in sec-ret. The representation was by states and the average attendance throughout the convention was only a little more than thirty.

     George Washington was chosen as presiding offi-cer of the Convention. Personalityand [Personality and] character played a large part in the discussions and debates at the Convention. In such a small group, person-ality counted as much as weighty argument.

     James Madison was, next to Washington, the most important member of the Virginian delegation.

     Gouverneur Morris and Ben Franklin were the im-portant delegates from Pennsylvania. The first plan proposed was the Virginia Plan which called for a completely new government.

     The principal [principle] features were the organisation [organization] of a legislature of two houses with increased powers, the establishment of a separate executive, and the creation of an independent judiciary.

     To check this drift toward radical change, New Jersey and Connecticut presented the New Jersey Plan which was in sharp contrast to the Virginia plan [Plan] and called for only a revision of the Arti-cles of Confederation. After a short discussion, however, the Virginia plan [Plan] won.

1946/1946_22.jpg

     The dividing line at the Convention soon became noticeable. It was the same one as in the Congress of Confederation- - the line between the small states and the large states. On July 16, the great com-promise of the Constitution was made. There were to be two houses in Congress. The lower with re-presentation according to the population and the upper with each state having two votes. After this was accepted, the Convention proceeded rapidly and on much hotter terms.

     A committee of five was named to frame the Con-stitution. This committee made use of the New Jersey Plan, the Virginia Plan, the Articles of Confederation, and for the rest it depended large-ly upon the constitutions of the states. They drew up the draft in ten days.

     The one clause referring to Congress in the Constitution that is the most important is found in Article I, Section 8, part 18, which gives Con-gress authority to make any laws it thinks are necessary to carry on the government.

     The Constitution provided for a Federal Supreme Court to settle all matters involving the inter-pretation of the law. The Court assumed a great power not actually provided for in the Constitu-tion. This was the authority to declare laws of Congress null and void.

     The Constitution also provided for the single executive. In making the provision, the Constitu-tion created an office which had never before been in existence.

     The constitution has an effective way of check-ing any over ambitious president. The Congress can overrule his veto by a two-thirds vote in both houses.

THE DREAM COMES TRUE

Katie Lehman

Gail glanced up to see Ron's smiling face. So many thoughts sprang to her mind. Thoughts that went way back to his football days at school. He had been so proud because he had been the star player on his team. After he had graduated, he had decided to join the Navy. Then he had been sent across and after three years he had come home again.

     It had been a long day, the day Ron came home. Everyone tried to do and say the right things. It was hard to know what to say for Ron had come home blind. Gail had marvelled [marveled] at the way he had kept his chin up and smiled all the time. But now she knew that he felt miserable inside. She had found out just yesterday. Ron had been sitting in the living room. The radio had been on and the announcer was telling about a star football player and the games he was playing in. Ron suddenly jumped up, turned off the radio and made his way the best he could from the room.

     Gail glanced at Ron again. He was smiling as he listened to the comedy play on the radio. "Oh why can't he be made to see?" She shut her eyes and kept repeating to herself, "Please make him see again, please." Gail couldn't sit and watch him any longer so she went up to bed.

1946/1946_48.jpg

     Sleep slowly overtook her and she fell asleep to dream. She heard music, sweet bell-like music. It came from the stars in the sky. The music became louder and Ron appeared. He was in his football suit and carried his football in his hand. A bright star shone on his face and she could see that he could see again. A voice softly said, "He shall see."

     "He can see, he can see," she cried. Then suddenly she awoke. She could hear her mother saying, "My son, oh my son."

     She jumped out of bed and hurried down stairs. Ron was smiling. His eyes were half opened. He didn't say anything but just stood there smiling. Then she remembered what the doctor had said. They were to call him right away if anything happened. She knew her mother was much too happy to remember to do it, so she walked quietly to the phone and called him.

     Both Ron and her mother were standing there in a daze when Dr. Green came. When Ron saw the doctor, he became filled with silence as the doctor analyzed the case. He looked up. "It's amazing, the object has moved to such a position that we can safely operate."

     Gail and her mother sat in the waiting room. Neither had much to say to each other. Soon the doctor came out. His eyes were filled with won-derment. He did not speak by they knew by the light in his eyes and the way he smiled, that it had been a success.

Note 1: Willa Cather was born December 7, 1873 in Virginia; however, she and her family moved to Nebraska in 1883. Cather graduated from the University of Nebraska-Lincoln in 1895 and, during her long writing career, became well-known for her writings about pioneering Nebraska (Ahearn).

Note 2: In 1905, Nebraska "outlawed the sale, manufacture, or distribution of cigarettes and cigarette papers" ("Anti-smoking Law of 1905"). This law did not restrict the actual smoking of cigarettes and said nothing about pipe tobacco or cigars (a more "socially acceptable" option). Thus smokers still found ways to acquire cigarettes. However, in 1919, the ban was repealed and replaced with "two new cigarette laws, 'Smoking cigarettes in public eating places, penalty' and 'Advertising cigarettes, penalty'" ("Anti-smoking Law of 1905). Again, only prohibiting cigarette smoking, and not tobacco use in general. However, the University of Nebraska-Lincoln prohibited smoking on campus, regardless of the form as early as 1907 ("Smokers on the Campus"). So this commentary on boys who smoke discusses an idea that is generally permitted, though technically not allowed at their school, which was located on the University of Nebraska-Lincoln's campus.

Note 3: Youth Week was sponsored by the United Christian Youth Movement, as noted in the author's text. The United Christian Youth Movement of North America began in 1934 (Leffler & Bond). According to a 1948 newspaper, it was under the supervision of the International Council of Religious Education (Venable). The United Christian Youth Movement sponsored many conferences for youth that sought to correct perceived societal ills such as race tension and poor leadership capabilities (Leffler & Bond). By 1952, the movement had switched organizational supervision to the National Council of Churches (Jameson).

Note 4: The "jitterbug" dance originated in the United States and became popular in the 1930s and 1940s. Its movements are "energetic, acrobatic, and improvisational" and can be "performed with a partner, solo, or in a line" (Stein). Its name comes from the "jerky movements" of dancers during the Prohibition who drank too much alcohol. This type of dance is most often performed with jazz and swing music that has 4/4 time, though other upbeat music such as rock'n'roll can work (Stein).

Note 5: This essay, as with many essays in this text, should be read with context. The student who wrote this work moved to Nebraska from a southern state which likely still had Jim Crow laws. As such, their previous experience with race vastly differed from their observations of racial interactions in Nebraska. Although these observations likely were not cohesive across every black/white interaction, it does bring to light that racial prejudice was slowly changing. Readers should notice that, despite comments that seem derogatory, this student's expression of ideas are fairly radical for their time.

Note 6: The Nebraska state "Home for Dependent Children" was originally established as the "Home for the Friendless" in 1881 and "took in destitute children and elderly women" (Nelson). Though established by the state legislature, it was a private organization until 1897 when it was taken over by the state (Nelson). Children who were "abandoned, neglected, or committed by the court" were "placed by the Home with adoptive or foster families" ("Youngest and...").The home regularly placed 100 or more children each year and housed between 200 and 300 children ("Youngest and..."). In 1906, the Nebraska Orthopedic Hospital took over one floor of the home. In 1909, the name changed to the "State Public School" and in 1911 to "Home for Dependent Children." With this final name change, the home was temporarily moved from the southeast corner of South and 11st Streets in Lincoln, NE to 944 H Street because the hospital took over the entire building. The home moved again in 1914 to 1425 F Street (Nelson). By 1946, it is evident that the home had moved again, this time to the address the author notes: 5903 Walker Avenue.

Note 7: Jazz is a type of music that relies heavily on improvisation. Although there is much variety in jazz, it tends to be rhythmic and include "swing" forward momentum. Jazz music often has call and repeat type sounds where one instrument might respond to another instrument's notes. Because of the personal nature of jazz, it is often emotion-filled and unique to the artist ("What is Jazz?").

Note 8: The author of this essay mixed up the Roman and Greek gods. The student likely learned about both of these civilizations around the same time in class. Since the Roman culture appropriated many of the Greek customs, including their dieties, the student likely misremembered which diety names were the Greek version and which were the Roman version. As such, they put the Roman names in their essay. The corrected Greek version are noted.

Note 9: This work is a parody of William Shakespeare's Macbeth. Macbeth is one of Shakespeare's shorter tragedies and was likely written between 1599 and 1606. The play is originally set in Scotland and follows Scottish general Macbeth as he hungers for political power. Macbeth and his wife Lady Macbeth both spiral downward as they encounter the consequences of greed ("Macbeth by William Shakespeare"). The author of this parody likely read the play in class, and then chose to create an alternate version of the text, sometimes staying close to the original and sometimes drifting away.

Note 10: "Aide de camp" means "a subordinate military or naval officer acting as a confidential assistant to a superior, usually to a general officer or admiral" ("aide-de-camp"). In the context of this text, the author is likely insinuating that she feels like her mother's subordinate who is forced to help in the kitchen, even though she'd rather not.

Note 11: Although "gay" started showing up in written psychological works in the 1940s, it wasn't until the 1970s that the word become a more common term for "a (usually male) homosexual" ("gay (adj.)"). Before it became more common to discuss sexual orientation in the 1970s, "gay" described a person who was "full of joy, merry; light-hearted, carefree" or someone who was "wanton, lewd, lascivious" ("gay (adj.)"). For this text, the meaning is likely the first archaic definition, describing a joyous or carefree feeling. For more information on the etymology of the term "gay," visit www.etymonline.com/word/gay
